About Me

✨💡😎

Hi, my name is Ziqi Liu, and you can call me Queenie. I am a Sydney-based interaction design student who enjoys turning research insights into clear, testable, and visually engaging digital experiences. My practice sits between user research, prototyping, and visual storytelling. I am interested in everyday technologies — from health and travel tools to AI-related communication experiences — and how design can make them feel more understandable, supportive, and human. With a background that bridges media, communication, and interaction design, I care not only about how a product looks, but also how it speaks, behaves, and guides people through an experience. I am currently developing my skills in product and interaction design, with a focus on usability, emotional tone, and the connection between technology and visual craft.

MedEasy

A medication support app designed to help older adults with chronic conditions scan, review, edit, remind and share medication information with more confidence.

Health Interface Older Adults Accessibility Usability Testing Iteration
Overview

Bridging the gap between elderly users and digital medication management

MedEasy was designed through a process of secondary research, lo-fi wireframing, and iterative usability testing to reduce the cognitive load placed on older adults managing complex medication regimens.

Context Individual Project
My Role UX/UI Designer
Research, wireframing, usability testing, visual design, iteration
Timeline Aug 2025 — Nov 2025
Tools Figma, FigJam, Miro
01 / Challenge

Challenges

Target Users

Older adults aged 60+ with chronic conditions. Including users with bilingual needs, low digital confidence, vision issues, or tremor

01 / Reducing refill errors

Older users may accidentally tap the wrong area or miss important details during a refill.

02 / Making medical information understandable

Medication names, dosage, pharmacy details, and warnings need to be clear without overwhelming the user.

03 / Supporting different abilities and contexts

The interface needed to work across languages, low confidence, vision changes, tremor, and unstable internet connection.

02 / Process

Design Process

Timeline from research to iteration.

01 Research

Analyze forums and communities to understand common barriers that elderly people face when using digital tools.

02 Insights

Core requirements: reduced steps, large-sized controls, clear language, and better feedback.

03 Wireframes

Create low fidelity prototypes for login, homepage, dispensing steps, reminders, and personal information.

04 Testing

Remote verbal thinking test with four participants（Think-aloud sessions）

05 Iteration

Simplify visual guidance, reminder location, and offline feedback based on user behavior.

Key Features

04 / Iteration

What I Changed

Before → After

Before

✕ Unclear starting point

✕ Icon-only navigation (hard to understand)

✕ Reminder actions were missed

✕ Small reminder pickers

After

✓ Clear "Tap to scan" cue

✓ Icon + text navigation

✓ Reminder/Calendar moved closer to success message

✓ Larger date/time controls

05 / Reflection

Learnings

Small details such as labels, feedback, touch size, and action placement can strongly affect whether older users feel safe continuing a task.

This project helped me understand that accessibility is not only about adding features, but about reducing uncertainty at each step. For older adults, a successful interface needs to feel predictable, forgiving, and respectful. My biggest design decision was to make the refill process narrower, not broader: fewer steps, clearer actions, and stronger feedback.

AirPal

AirPal is a wearable airport companion for first-time solo travellers. It combines quick-access storage, a foldable screen, real-time guidance and support access to make airport journeys clearer and less stressful.

Physical-Digital Travel UX Concept Design User Testing
My Role Physical-digital interaction designer

I contributed to concept development, physical form decisions and testing interpretation. My strongest contribution was connecting the wearable bag form with the on-screen guidance system, so AirPal felt like support within an airport journey rather than just a screen attached to a product.

Team Context Group project with Cassie Xu and Henry Fried
Timeline Mar–May 2026
Tools & Methods Sketching, Blender, physical mock-up, storyboard, role-play testing, SUS, interview feedback
00 / My Contribution

What I Personally Did

01 / Concept Direction

Helped shape AirPal as a wearable crossbody bag instead of only a screen-based apps.

02 / Physical Prototype Thinking

Explored the waist bag form, 90° foldable screen, quick-access storage, and light/vibration alerts through sketches and product-detail thinking.

03 / Testing Reflection

Helped interpret testing feedback.

01 / Problem

Why AirPal?

First-time solo travel is not only about finding information. It is about knowing what to do next.

Airports can overwhelm first-time solo travellers with unfamiliar layouts, time pressure, security rules, gate changes and limited support. Our design challenge was to reduce uncertainty by making the next action visible, simple and reassuring.

02 / Concept

Wearable Airport Companion

A physical-digital solution, not just another phone interface.

AirPal is a crossbody / waist bag with a foldable glanceable screen. It keeps passports, boarding passes, cards and phones close, while providing journey guidance, flight updates, navigation prompts and support options.

Secure Storage

Keeps key travel items easy to reach during security and boarding.

90° Foldable Screen

Allows glanceable viewing while walking, standing or waiting.

Multimodal Alerts

Uses light, vibration and audio to notify users in noisy airport environments.

04 / Testing

How We Tested It

Scenario-based testing helped us see how users acted under pressure.

We tested AirPal with 5 proxy users in a busy user testing fair environment. The methods included think-aloud, role-play tasks, SUS survey and short interviews.

Task 1 / Security Check

Users followed on-screen guidance to remove the correct items under time pressure.

Task 2 / Gate Navigation

Users followed instructions to reach the boarding gate, but distance-only directions were confusing.

Task 3 / Passport Lost

Users responded quickly to the alert and chose support options to solve the problem.

SUS Result 72.5

Good overall usability, with room to simplify navigation and menus.

Testing Insight
  • Insight 1 — Navigation needed landmarks
  • Insight 2 — Support needed to be visible earlier
  • Insight 3 — The interface needed stronger hierarchy

05 / Interface Iteration

What Changed

From messy screens to clearer guidance.

Before

✕ Too much information in one screen

✕ Support was less visible

✕ Direction steps were too long

✕ Users could lose track of current progress

After

✓ Divided interface into Journey, Navigation and Support

✓ Made staff help, translation and peer support easier to access

✓ Simplified directions into shorter steps

✓ Added progress indicators to show current stage

06 / Future Direction

Next Improvements

What I would improve if the project continued.

Contextual Navigation

Use landmarks like “towards Starbucks” instead of only metres and arrows.

Simpler Menu Hierarchy

Group functions into Journey, Navigation and Support to reduce decision-making pressure.

Emotional Support

Add calming materials, friendly feedback and small rewards to make the journey feel less stressful.

07 / Reflection

What I Learned

Useful design is not about adding more functions. It is about making the next step clearer.

AirPal helped me understand how physical form and interface design need to work together. My key learning was that in stressful environments, a product should reduce choices, guide one action at a time, and provide reassurance before users feel lost.
